To go from zero to mastery of the concepts required for the Junior Mathematical Challenge (JMC), Junior Kangaroo, and Junior Mathematical Olympiad (JMO), students should follow a structured 4-week program focusing on mental agility, geometric deduction, algebraic modeling, and the rigorous art of proof.
Week 1: Number Theory and Arithmetic Foundations
Focus on numerical properties and mental calculations, as calculators and measuring instruments are strictly forbidden in all these tests
.
Primes and Factors: Master the definition of prime numbers and practice identifying them up to 100
. Learn to use prime factorization to identify all factors of a number
.
Divisibility Rules: Memorize and apply rules for 3, 4, 9, and 11 to solve complex multiplication and division problems quickly
.
Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages: Practice converting between these forms and performing operations without a calculator
.
Powers and Roots: Mastery of square and cube numbers is essential for "crossnumber" puzzles and sequence problems
.
Week 2: Geometry and Measurement
Study the properties of shapes, moving beyond simple measurement to logical deduction.
Angle Facts: Master the theorems for angles on a straight line, vertically opposite angles, and interior/exterior angles of triangles and polygons
.
Area and Perimeter: Practice calculating these for squares, rectangles, and triangles, as well as complex shaded regions formed by overlapping shapes
.
Symmetry and Nets: Develop the ability to visualize rotational and reflectional symmetry and identify which 2D nets can form 3D solids like cubes
.
Circles and Semicircles: Learn to calculate areas and perimeters of circles, often leaving answers in terms of π
.
Week 3: Algebra, Sequences, and Logical Reasoning
Learn to translate word problems into mathematical models and solve puzzles.
Equations: Practice forming and solving linear and simultaneous equations from text descriptions, such as those involving ages or the costs of items
.
Sequences: Recognize and define rules for number sequences, including those where each term is the sum of previous terms
.
Logic Puzzles: Solve "magic squares" where rows and columns sum to the same total, and practice "truth-teller and liar" logic problems
.
Speed, Distance, and Time: Mastery of these calculations is a frequent requirement for word problems
.
Week 4: Presentation, Proof, and Olympiad Mastery
The final week transitions from finding the correct answer to proving why it is correct, which is the core requirement of the JMO Section B
.
Full Written Explanations: Practice writing solutions in extended prose, explaining every logical step rather than just providing a value
.
Using Algebra for Proof: Use variables to express connections simply and declare those variables fully (e.g., "Let n be the smallest integer...")
.
Proving Uniqueness: Mastery involves showing that your found solution is the only one possible and that all other cases have been eliminated
.
Mock Exams: Complete past papers under strict time limits—60 minutes for the JMC/Kangaroo and 2 hours for the JMO—focusing on accuracy over guessing
.
Pro Tip: Use the "For Investigation" problems found in extended solution booklets to deepen your understanding of core concepts through further exploration
